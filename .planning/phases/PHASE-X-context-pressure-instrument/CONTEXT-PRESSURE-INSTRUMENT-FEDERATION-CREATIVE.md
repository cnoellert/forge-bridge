## The experience thesis — the contract is honest-absence, federated

The project has carried one experience thread since CR.1: **honest absence over silent miss.** "I couldn't resolve that" beats a confident wrong answer; a typed decline beats a silent fallback. The room has paid for this thread repeatedly — the `unreachable_api` / `unreconstructable_guard` reason codes in capture-spec §4, the no-synthesis law on mutations, the "orchestrator may terminate but does not impersonate" doctrine.

What DT's surfacing actually found is that **the federation already shares this exact value, and shipped a vocabulary for it.** ADR-000 Rule 6 is not an architecture rule that happens to touch experience — read it as an experience contract: *"A capability MUST surface unresolved or ambiguous references as typed outcomes rather than silently falling back."* That sentence is the artist's protection against the worst failure mode in a deictic interface — the system doing *something* to the wrong object because it guessed. `references.py` is that protection given a shape: `status`, `candidates`, `reason_code`, `message`.

So my top-line read: **aligning the resolver to `ReferenceResolution` does not impose a federation-shaped abstraction the artist never asked for. It imposes the artist's own ask, finally typed.** The contract's vocabulary is *richer and more honest* than any bridge-internal shape we'd have drafted, because it forces three operator-facing distinctions a homegrown bool/enum would have collapsed: resolved-vs-unresolved-vs-ambiguous (three different operator experiences, not one "fail"), and the `reason_code`/`message` split (machine-branchable cause vs. human-readable sentence). That's not a leak. That's the experience we've been hand-rolling, standardized.

**One guard, though, and it's the whole of my caution:** the contract is a *good shape*; it is not yet a *good sentence*. `message: str | None = None` is nullable and unconstrained. A contract field existing does not make the operator's experience good — it makes it *possible*. The honest-absence thread dies just as dead from a populated `reason_code` with `message=None` as it does from a silent fallback, because the artist gets `reason_code="unresolvable_ambiguous"` and no idea what to do next. **The contract gives us the envelope for honest decline; authoring the decline is still our job, and it's the job that matters.** (This is the `expected_well_formed` lesson one more turn down: well-formedness — a valid `ReferenceResolution` — precedes content — a `message` that helps. Don't let the contract's existence launder the content work.)

## Redline of DT's three positions

### Position 1 — "Do NOT re-scope Phase X." **ENDORSE, unconditionally.**

Nothing in the experience changes what the instrument *measures*. The artist never sees the instrument; they see the resolver the instrument might gate. Re-scoping the measurement because we found its output has a federation name would be the exact victory-lap inversion the room guards against — and worse, it would put architecture-elegance ahead of the operator by spending Phase X's budget on the contract rather than on the friction. The blind Q3 threshold is the operator's protection against us building a resolver they don't need. Leave it. The reframe is vocabulary, not mission. **No redline.**

### Position 2 — "The resolver emits `ReferenceResolution`." **ENDORSE — and sharpen: this is an experience win, not just a migration saving.**

DT frames this as "costs us nothing now and saves a parallel-vocabulary migration later." That's the *architecture* argument and it's correct. But DT **under-weighted the experience opportunity** — he sold the contract as cost-avoidance when its real value to the artist is the three fields he doesn't mention in Position 2 at all: `candidates`, `reason_code`, `message`.

Here is the sharpening. A bridge-internal resolver shape, left to our own devices under deadline, tends toward `{resolved: bool, value: str}` — because that's all the *compile path* needs to inject a referent. That shape is a silent-miss generator: `resolved=False` tells the artist nothing, and `resolved=True` with the wrong value is the ratify-caught friction case with no machine-readable trail explaining *why* it picked wrong. **`ReferenceResolution` structurally prevents us from shipping that thin shape**, because the contract makes `unresolved` and `ambiguous` first-class siblings of `resolved`, each carrying explanatory payload. The contract is a forcing function *toward* the honest-absence experience and *away* from the bool we'd have reached for under pressure.

So: endorse Position 2, but record it correctly. **The reason to emit `ReferenceResolution` is not migration thrift — it's that the contract makes the honest-decline experience the path of least resistance instead of the path we have to discipline ourselves into.** State it that way in §8 so the next author doesn't optimize the contract back down to a bool because "that's all compile needs."

### Position 3 — "op→type becomes pipeline-declared capability metadata." **ENDORSE THE DIRECTION, CONTEST THE TIMING — and DT under-weighted a real experience cost.**

The *direction* is right and it's partly my own read (hooks-as-ontology, §2.4): the hook *is* the guard, the guard semantics live in pipeline, and ADR-000 Rule 3's smell test ("could this information be declared by the capability instead?") answers yes — deriving §2.3 by reading `forge-pipeline/flame_hooks/forge_tools/` is an ADR-000 no-sibling-internals violation we should name as interim. All true. **Endorse that this is the clean form.**

But DT's pass reads as though declaring-the-metadata is the obvious next move, and **it under-weights the §2.4 heterogeneous-guard problem against its own conclusion.** Here is the experience cost DT skips:

The guard is *not uniformly* `op → required_type`. §2.4 establishes three guard styles: nominal (`isinstance`), behavioral/duck-typed (`hasattr(s, "record_in")` for `forge_switch_grade`), and cardinality-constrained (`len(segs) == 1`). A *declared* `op→type` metadata field can faithfully carry the nominal case. **It cannot faithfully carry the duck-typed or cardinality cases without either (a) pipeline declaring a far richer predicate grammar than `CapabilityDeclaration` has today, or (b) lossy flattening of "satisfies `hasattr(record_in)`" down to "type `PySegment`" — which is exactly the mis-type §2.4 warns produces wrong referents.**

Why this is an experience problem, not just a modeling one: a flattened declaration that says `forge_switch_grade requires PySegment` when the live guard is `hasattr(s, "record_in")` will, on some real selection, **disagree with what the operator actually sees in the menu.** The tool is in the menu (the live guard passed) but the resolver's flattened metadata says no-such-type, OR the resolver thinks it can resolve and the live guard would have declined. Either way the artist experiences the resolver and the menu telling them different things about the same selection. That's a trust fracture — the worst kind, because deictic interfaces live entirely on the artist trusting that "this" means what they pointed at.

**So my redline on Position 3 is a timing-and-fidelity gate, not a reversal:** the op→type-as-capability-metadata move is the right destination *for the nominal-guard ops* (which is the dominant `PySequence` rename class — R3/R4, the whole point of Phase X). For the duck-typed and cardinality ops, **declaring the metadata is blocked on a richer predicate grammar that doesn't exist in the contract yet, and inventing it is out of Phase X scope.** Don't let "op→type becomes capability metadata" land as a blanket; land it as "**for the nominal-guard ops, declared; for the heterogeneous-guard ops, the guard stays where it lives (the hook) until the contract can express predicates without flattening.**" Which leads directly to the second Creative ask.

## Creative ask (a) — disambiguation surface: design now, or defer past the first resolver?

**Position: DEFER the disambiguation *surface*; CAPTURE the `ambiguous` signal from record one. These are different things and conflating them is the trap.**

Two separable decisions hide inside "do we handle ambiguity":

1. **Does the instrument/resolver *recognize* ambiguity** (≥2 selections satisfy the guard, no clear dominant) and emit `status="ambiguous"` with `candidates[]`?
2. **Does the operator-facing surface *offer the candidates back*** — "did you mean 30sec_edit 21 or 30sec_edit 22?" — as an interactive disambiguation affordance?

**On (1): yes, from record one — it's free and it's measurement.** The capture model already enumerates the selection set (`selection_typed: [{type, name}]`, capture-spec §3). Recognizing "≥2 of these satisfy the op's guard" is a count, not a feature. And it is *exactly the kind of contextual-failure the instrument exists to measure* — an ambiguous deictic is a distinct failure family from `wrong_referent` and `unresolved_reference`. If we collapse ambiguous into "unresolved" at capture time, we **blind the Q3 measurement to a whole failure class**, and we can't get it back without re-capture (the CR.1 "can't measure what you didn't record" lesson, §4 of Orch's pass). The `ambiguous` + `candidates` *recording* is non-negotiable and costs nothing. Map it as capture-spec §8 already does: multiple-selection-can't-disambiguate → `status="ambiguous"` + `candidates`.

**On (2): defer the interactive surface past the first resolver — and the Q3 ratify-CAUGHT framing is exactly why deferral is safe.** Here's the reasoning, and it's the operator's safety net doing real work:

The Q3 doc (§2) establishes that contextual-resolution failures are **ratify-CAUGHT, not dangerous** — a wrong referent reaches *preview*, the operator sees the wrong value at ratify, and rejects. The cost is *friction* (re-prompt), not corruption. Now apply that to ambiguity. The first resolver's honest behavior on `ambiguous` is **decline → "I'm not sure which one — say it more specifically"** (a `status="ambiguous"` rendered as honest decline, no apply). The artist re-prompts or selects one and retries. That is *friction*, yes — but it is the **same class and roughly the same magnitude of friction** as the ratify-reject path the room already accepted as the safety net. Building an interactive "did you mean X or Y?" disambiguation surface *now* would be spending real UX-design budget to convert a one-step re-prompt into a one-click pick — a genuine improvement, but a **second-order friction reduction on top of a safety net the room already deemed acceptable.** That is precisely the long-tail chasing the Q3 cost-anchor (§2, §3 MORE-DATA/REJECT zones) tells us *not* to fund before the data justifies it.

There's a sharper reason to defer the surface specifically: **we don't yet know the frequency of the ambiguous case.** The whole instrument exists because we refuse to build resolvers on intuition. Designing the disambiguation UI before the corpus tells us how often `ambiguous` actually fires would be building the surface blind — the exact sin Phase X is structured to prevent, just one failure-class over. If the re-capture shows `ambiguous` is 2% of contextual failures, an interactive picker is wasted craft; if it's 30%, it's clearly worth designing *and we'll know the candidate-set shapes to design for.*

**The honest-decline IS the first-resolver disambiguation surface.** `status="ambiguous"` + a `message` like *"2 selected sequences match — I can't tell which 'this' you mean; select one or name it"* is a complete, honest, ship-able experience. It tells the truth, it puts the artist back in control, it never guesses. The interactive candidate-picker is a *later legibility upgrade* gated on measured frequency — which is the same posture we took on every other surface. **Capture ambiguous now; decline honestly on it in the first resolver; design the picker when the corpus ranks it.**

(Redline note to DT: your ask (a) framed this as a binary "design now or defer." The operative split isn't design-vs-defer — it's *record-vs-render*. Record from day one, render when measured. Stating it as a binary risks the room deferring the *recording* too, which would be a measurement hole.)

## Creative ask (b) — does declaring op→type shift where the resolver lives?

**Position: the resolver lives in BRIDGE — but the *guard-evaluation* it depends on splits by guard style, and the heterogeneous case (§2.4) argues for keeping that evaluation in the hook, not relocating the whole resolver to pipeline.**

Let me separate two things DT's ask runs together: *where the resolver lives* and *where the guard semantics live.* They are not the same component.

**The resolver lives in bridge. This is ADR-000 load-bearing and it's an experience requirement, not just an architecture one.** Rule 3: Bridge orchestrates, discovers, plans, remembers. Resolving "this sequence" → a referent is a *planning* act — it's bridge turning the artist's deictic NL into an identifier a sibling op can consume (Phase 5/7: "plan by declared capability → invoke"). It belongs to the conversational runtime that already owns compile/preview/ratify. Pushing the resolver into pipeline would make pipeline the home for *orchestration* logic — a Rule 3 violation, a new center of mass forming. And experientially: the artist talks to bridge (chat, Console). The thing that interprets "this" must live next to the thing they're talking to, so it can decline honestly *in the same conversation* rather than round-tripping a federation hop to find out it can't resolve. Honest decline has to be *fast and local* or it stops feeling honest and starts feeling broken.

**But the guard *evaluation* is where §2.4 bites, and it does shift the answer for the heterogeneous case.** The resolver's core question is: "does this selected object satisfy the op's guard?" For nominal guards (`isinstance` — the dominant `PySequence` class), bridge can evaluate this itself from a *declared* `op→type` metadata field: it has the selection (`selection_typed`, pull-clean per §4/§5), it has the declared required type, it compares. Clean. Resolver in bridge, metadata declared by pipeline, **and this covers R3/R4 — the entire dominant op class Phase X is about.**

For the duck-typed and cardinality guards, the snapshot **may not reliably reconstruct the predicate** (§4 reconstructability (b); the `hasattr(record_in)` and `len==1` cases). This is the heterogeneous-guard problem, and it argues for **the guard evaluation living where the predicate natively lives — the hook**, which *is* the guard and inherits Flame's exact selection + classification (capture-spec §5.1 archaeology: "the hook already POSSESSES the exact predicate semantics"). So the federation-clean architecture is **not** "resolver in bridge vs. resolver in pipeline." It's:

- **Resolver (planning + the `ReferenceResolution` emission + the honest decline) → bridge.** Always. Experience requires it local.
- **Guard evaluation → split by declared guard style.** Nominal guards: bridge evaluates from declared `op→type` metadata (clean, covers the dominant case). Heterogeneous guards (duck-typed/cardinality): bridge *delegates the predicate check* to where the guard lives — pipeline declaring not just a type but a resolvable predicate, OR (interim, honest) bridge emits `status="unresolved"` + `reason_code="unreconstructable_guard"` rather than flatten-and-guess.

**Does the heterogeneous-guard problem argue for one home over the other?** It argues for *neither pure home* — it argues for a **bridge resolver with a typed honest-decline escape hatch for the guards it can't reconstruct.** And that is *exactly* what `ReferenceResolution.reason_code` is for. The §2.4 problem, which looks like an architecture headache ("where do we put the duck-typed predicate"), is **dissolved by the contract into an experience decision**: when bridge can't reconstruct the guard, it doesn't move the resolver to pipeline and it doesn't flatten-and-guess — it emits `unresolved` + `reason_code="unreconstructable_guard"` + a `message` the operator can act on. **The honest-absence pattern is the answer to the heterogeneous-guard problem.** Bridge resolves what it can prove (nominal, dominant), and *honestly declines* what it can't (heterogeneous, tail) — rather than relocating itself to chase predicate fidelity it can earn later by pipeline declaring richer guards.

This also fully vindicates my Position-3 redline: declared `op→type` metadata is the right home **for the nominal ops**, and the heterogeneous ops ride the `reason_code` decline until the contract grows a predicate grammar. The resolver never moves. The guard fidelity grows incrementally. The artist always gets either a resolved referent or an honest, actionable decline — never a flattened guess.

## The opportunity DT left on the floor — `reason_code` / `message` is the honest-decline experience, name it as a deliverable

DT's mapping table (surfacing doc) sends `unreachable_api` / `unreconstructable_guard` → `reason_code` and stops. He treats `reason_code` as a *bucket for the failure cases we already named.* That's the architecture read. The **experience** read is that `reason_code` (machine-branchable) + `message` (human sentence) is the single most artist-load-bearing pair in the contract, and it's the place the room's recurring honest-absence value either gets delivered or quietly dropped.

The distinction matters and the contract drew it deliberately (`references.py:41–42`): `reason_code: str | None` and `message: str | None` are *separate fields*. `reason_code` is for the Console/CLI to branch on (render the ambiguous-picker vs. the re-prompt hint vs. the "no selection — select something first" nudge). `message` is the sentence the artist reads. A resolver that populates `reason_code` and leaves `message=None` satisfies the contract and **fails the artist** — they get a code, not a sentence. We have been here before: this is *"chat returns dispatch envelopes, not human answers"* (the v1.9 thesis, `chat_is_a_console_not_chat`), recurring one layer down at the resolver. A `reason_code` with no `message` is a dispatch envelope. The honest-absence thread demands the sentence.

**So I'm naming a deliverable DT's pass doesn't:** if/when the resolver builds, **`message` is authored content, not a passthrough of `reason_code`,** and the small set of `reason_code` values is an operator-experience vocabulary we design (no-selection / ambiguous-multi / unreconstructable-guard / unresolved-name), each with a `message` template that tells the artist *what to do next*, not just what went wrong. That's the difference between "I can't resolve that" (a wall) and "2 sequences are selected — pick one or name it" (a door). The contract gives us both fields *precisely so we can build the door.* Don't let it ship as a wall with a status code on it.

(This is additive and respects Phase X scope — it's a note on the *resolver* if Q3 clears BUILD, not a re-scope of the *instrument*. But it needs to be on the record now so the resolver author doesn't treat `message` as optional just because the pydantic field is.)

## The single experience principle to carry into the resolver decision

**The contract gives us the shape of honest decline; it does not give us the decline itself — and the decline is the experience.** `ReferenceResolution` is the right envelope precisely because it makes `unresolved` and `ambiguous` first-class, refusing to let us collapse them into a silent bool. But a valid `ReferenceResolution` with `message=None` is a dispatch envelope, not an answer — the v1.9 failure recurring one layer down. Resolve what we can prove (nominal guards, the dominant rename class), **honestly decline — in a sentence the artist can act on — everything we can't** (ambiguity, unreconstructable guards, missing selection), and never flatten-and-guess to fake a resolution the safety net would only have to catch. The artist must always get a referent they pointed at or a door they can walk through — never a confident wrong guess, and never a status code standing in for a sentence.
